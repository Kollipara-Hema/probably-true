    Chapter 2 · The trick of conditional thinking

    Why does spam end up in your inbox?

    Your spam filter is 95% accurate — that sounds great. Yet real emails still get flagged, and real spam still gets through. How? The math has a twist most people miss.

    Big idea: most people answer the wrong question. "How accurate is the filter?" and "Is this message actually spam?" sound like the same question. They are not.
    Real spam in your inbox: about 1 in 100 messages.
    Filter accuracy: 95%.
    A message gets flagged: should you trust it?
    A message just got flagged as spam. How likely is it that it actually is spam?
    Reveal: if real spam is rare in your inbox, the answer can be as low as 16%. A 95%-accurate filter is wrong about most of what it flags. The next section shows you why — visually, before any math.

    Multiplication · The trick of "at least one"

    Tiny chances compound faster than you'd guess

    Roll a die four times — chance of at least one six? Most people guess "around four out of six." The real answer is 52%. The trick: don't compute "at least one" directly. Compute the chance of none and subtract from 1.

    We'll explore this through three doors — same idea, three different ways of seeing it.

    Addition · Why you can't just add

    The overlap gets counted twice

    If 30% of people are tall and 30% wear glasses, then 60% are tall or wear glasses? Only if no tall people wear glasses. Otherwise the overlap got counted in both groups — once too many.

    Drag either circle. Watch P(A) + P(B) drift apart from P(A ∪ B) every time the circles overlap. The gap between them is the overlap, exactly.

    Now the math makes sense

    Bayes' rule, visually

    When you slid the spam and accuracy sliders earlier, you were doing Bayesian reasoning. The formula on the right isn't algebra — it's a recipe for the picture you already saw.

    The whole rule is just "of the bordered tiles, what fraction are red?" That's it. Bayes' theorem is the bookkeeping for that question.
    The question you actually want answered
    P(spam | flagged) = P(flagged | spam) × P(spam) ⁄ P(flagged)
    Numerator: real spam that got flagged — the red bordered tiles.
    Denominator: everything that got flagged — every bordered tile, pink or red.
    Final synthesis

    Five things that survive this chapter

    Once these settle, you've got the whole conditional-probability toolkit — overlap, compounding, and reversed conditionals.

    1
    If events overlap, you can't just add their probabilities. Subtract the overlap once.
    2
    For "at least one," compute P(none) and subtract from 1. Tiny chances compound fast.
    3
    P(A given B) is not P(B given A). Reversing the conditional changes the answer.
    4
    Filter accuracy alone is not enough — base rates matter. A great filter for a rare event is mostly false alarms.
    5
    Bayes' rule is just bookkeeping for "of all the flags, what fraction are real?"
