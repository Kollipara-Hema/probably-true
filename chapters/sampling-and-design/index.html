        Chapter 03 · Sampling
        Chapter 3 · The most underrated source of error

        A bigger sample isn’t always a better one.

        A school cafeteria owner wants to add one new item to next year’s menu. Budget is tight — he can pick exactly one. So he runs a survey.

        He stands at the counter at lunchtime for a whole week and asks every kid who comes through: “if we added one new item, what would you want?”

        By Friday, he has 2,847 responses. The winner: pizza slices, by a landslide.

        The sample is huge. The number is decisive. Should he invest?
        Most people say yes. 2,847 is a big number. But the question to ask isn’t how many kids he heard from. It’s which kids — and that’s a completely different property of the survey. Look right →
        Two ways the same survey could be run
        Cafeteria A
        surveyed only kids in the lunch line · 2,847 responses · one section of the school
        winner: pizza · biased
        Cafeteria B
        random mix from across the school · 300 responses · cafeteria, classrooms, lawn, packed lunches
        winner: salad bowl · representative
        size doesn’t fix bias. a wrong answer measured precisely is still wrong.

        Act 2 · Five ways to grab

        A basket of fruit. Five ways to grab.

        Random was the answer in Act 1. But random from where? Real life rarely lets you shake the whole world in a basket.

        Pick a strategy. Grab 20 fruits. See how close your handful matches what’s actually in the basket — and then run it again. The pattern that emerges isn’t about which technique is “best.” It’s about how each one is wrong.

        Run each technique 5–10 times. Notice the personalities: convenience is wrong the same way every time. simple random is wrong differently every time. cluster swings wildly. Each technique has a temperament.
        The real basket has 40% apples, 25% oranges, 15% bananas, 12% grapes, 8% pears. The closer your sample bars match the truth bars, the better your method worked.

        Act 3 · Sampling tells you who. It can’t tell you why.

        The coffee that didn’t cause anything.

        A perfect random sample of adults shows: coffee drinkers live longer than non-drinkers. Six years longer, on average. The sample is representative. The data is real.

        Should you start drinking more coffee?

        Toggle to experiment mode — where coffee gets assigned at random, not just observed — and the effect collapses. The coffee drinkers were just wealthier all along. Wealth is the lurking variable doing all the work.
        Sampling chooses who. Assignment chooses why. Random is the magic word in both — but it’s solving two completely different problems.

        The synthesis · Naming what you discovered

        Bias is a personality. Variance is a mood.

        Now that you’ve felt them, the names are easy. Bias is wrong-the-same-way-every-time — a systematic miss in one direction. Variance is wrong-differently-every-time — random scatter around the truth.

        Every sampling technique has its own mix. Some are biased and steady. Some are unbiased but noisy. Some are both. The “best” technique depends on what you can afford and what you know about your population.

        The takeaway: before you trust any statistic, ask one thing — how did these people get into the dataset?

        Final synthesis

        Six things that survive this chapter

        Once these settle, you’ve got the foundation that protects you from the most common failure mode in statistics: trusting a number because it’s big.

        1
        Big ≠ representative. A huge sample with the wrong people in it is worse than a small one with the right mix.
        2
        Bias is a property of the process, not the size. You can’t N-your-way out of it.
        3
        Different techniques have different personalities — convenience is steadily wrong, random is noisily right, cluster swings.
        4
        Stratified = some from all. Cluster = all from some. Same starting move — grouping — different finishing move.
        5
        Sampling chooses who. Assignment chooses why. Random is the magic word in both.
        6
        Before you trust any statistic, ask: how did these people get into the dataset?
